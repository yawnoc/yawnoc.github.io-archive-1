Introduction

This is a passage written by 趙元任 (Cheeu Yün-yam) (Chao Yüan-jên) (Zhao Yuanren), a Chinese-American linguist, etc. It is written in literary Chinese, with every character pronounced 尸 (shi) up to tone in Mandarin.

Mandarin has a higher rate of homophones than say Cantonese, due to loss of entering tone among other phonological shifts. To recap, 四聲, or the four classical tone classes of Chinese, are:

  1. 平 (level)
  2. 上 (rising)
  3. 去 (departing)
  4. 入 (entering)

These are not the four tones of modern Mandarin. Although modern Mandarin numerically has four tones, they only fall under the first three classical tone classes:

  1. 陰平 (dark level)
  2. 陽平 (light level)
  3. 上 (rising)
  4. 去 (departing)

From the perspective of Western linguistics, entering tones are not separate tones because they are syllables which end abruptly in -p, -t or -k. However, the distinction is important in Chinese poetry, where balancing 平聲 (level tone) and 仄聲 (sloping tone, i.e. 上去入) is highly desirable. Historically, Mandarin and other northern varieties of Chinese underwent 入派三聲; after losing the ending consonants -p, -t and -k, entering tone words were redistributed amongst the other three tones. To give a contrived example,

實食十石時

meaning "it's really time to eat ten stones", reads in Mandarin

shí shí shí shí shí

but in Cantonese

shat9 shik9 shap9 shek9 shee4.

Now 趙元任 (Cheeu Yün-yam) (Chao Yüan-jên) (Zhao Yuanren) did not write 《施氏食獅史》 to compare the phonologies of Cantonese and Mandarin, but to demonstrate (with an extreme example) why literary Chinese should not be romanised. In any regional variant, Literary Chinese is a language to be read and comprehended with the eyes, rather than heard with the ears, though it may still be read aloud. (Romanising vernacular Chinese, however, is all right. 趙元任 (Cheeu Yün-yam) (Chao Yüan-jên) (Zhao Yuanren) and others developed "Gwoyeu Romatzyh", a romanisation system for Mandarin which uses spelling, rather than diacritics, to indicate different tones.)

Translation

Romanisation (F2): 音標 (yam peeu) (yin piao) (yin biao)

Chinese text taken from Page 143 of 趙元任's 《語言問題》 (published in 1968 by 臺灣商務印書館).

《施氏食獅史》
"An History of Mr Shih's Eating of Lions"
  • 施氏： Mr Shih; lit. he with surname Shih
  • 史： history; or record
石室詩士施氏，嗜獅，誓食十獅。
The poet Mr Shih of a stone house: he liked lions, and sware to eat ten lions.
  • 石： stone; or rock
  • 室： house; or room
  • 詩士： poet; lit. poem scholar
  • 嗜： liked; or was fond of
  • 誓： sware to; or took oath to
氏時時適市視獅。
Mr Shih went to the market all the time to view lions.
  • 氏
    Here, 施 has been dropped from 施氏.
  • 時時： all the time; or often
  • 適：往也； went to
十時，適十獅適市。是時，適施氏適市。
At ten o'clock, ten lions had just arrived at the market. At this time, Mr Shih had also just arrived at the market.
  • 十時： ten o'clock
    This is a modern term; perhaps "tenth hour" would be a better rendering. Classically, 時 means 時辰, which are named two-hour intervals rather than numbered one-hour intervals.
  • 適： had just; or happened to have just
  • 適：至也； arrived at
氏視是十獅，恃矢勢，使是十獅逝世。
Mr Shih saw these ten lions, and, relying on the situational advantage of the arrow, made these ten lions pass away.
  • 勢： situational advantage; or influence
  • 使： made; or caused
氏拾是十獅屍，適石室。
Mr Shih picked up the carcasses of these ten lions, and went to the stone house.
石室濕，氏使侍拭石室。
The stone house wet, Mr Shih made his servants wipe the stone house.
石室拭，氏始試食是十獅屍。
The stone house wiped, Mr Shih began to try eating the carcasses of these ten lions.
  • 始： began to; or started to
  • 試食： try eating; or try for taste; or taste test
食時，始識是十獅屍，實十石獅屍。
Only when eating, did he first know that the carcasses of these ten lions, were actually the carcasses of ten stone lions.
  • 始： first; or begin to
  • 實： actually; or in fact; or really
試釋是事。
Try to interpret these events.
  • 試： try to; or attempt to
  • 是事： these events; or this incident
