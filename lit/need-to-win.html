"The Need to Win" or
"Attaching Importance to the External"

First created: 20190415
Last modified: 20200217

Introduction

This "quote" of Chuang Tzŭ begins the chapter "Family" in Tim Winton's The Turning:

When an archer is shooting for nothing
He has all his skill
If he shoots for a brass buckle
He is already nervous
If he shoots for a prize of gold
He goes blind
Or sees two targets—
He is out of his mind!

His skill has not changed. But the prize
Divides him. He cares.
He thinks more of winning
Than of shooting—
And the need to win
Drains him of power.
Chuang Tzu

The passage above, entitled "The Need to Win", is from Thomas Merton's The Way of Chuang Tzu, which in Merton's own words, consists of "imitations" or "free interpretative readings" of the source material, rather than translations. Merton, a Trappist monk interested in Eastern religions, composed these interpretative readings after comparing "four of the best translations of Chuang Tzŭ into western languages, two English, one French, and one German".

The source of the passage is a dialogue between Confucius and one of his disciples in 《莊子・外篇・達生》, or "Understanding Life" in the "Outer Texts" of Chuang Tzŭ, a work traditionally credited to Chuang Tzŭ the Taoist philosopher. One of the English translations of this which Merton read was of course that of the great James Legge; see passage 4, "Yen Yuan asked Kung-nî ..." on Page 15 of The Texts of Taoism, Part II. As you shall see below, whether the original text actually refers to archery depends on how you interpret the word 注 (chü) (chu) (zhu).

In summary, the passage above is twice removed from the source material: once by translation (James Legge and others), and once through the interpretative reading of Thomas Merton. Just reading The Turning though, you wouldn't have any clue of this.

Chinese text taken from Chinese Text Project: 先秦兩漢・道家・莊子・外篇・達生.

顏淵問仲尼曰：「吾嘗濟乎觴深之淵，津人操舟若神。
顏淵 (Ngaan Yün) (Yen Yüan) (Yan Yuan) asked 仲尼 (Chung-nei) (Chung-ni) (Zhongni), saying, "I once crossed the deep waters of 觴深 (Shœng-sham) (Shang-shên) (Shangshen), and the men of the crossing handled boats like gods.
  • 顏淵 (Ngaan Yün) (Yen Yüan) (Yan Yuan)
    Confucius's favourite disciple. Also called 顏回 (Ngaan Wooi) (Yen Hui) (Yan Hui).
  • 仲尼 (Chung-nei) (Chung-ni) (Zhongni)
    The style name (or courtesy name) of Confucius.
  • 濟：渡也； crossed
  • 觴深 (Shœng-sham) (Shang-shên) (Shangshen)
    (Proper) name of a body of water.
    觴，音商， Cantonese: shœng1, Mandarin: shāng
  • 淵： the deep waters; or the deep
  • 操： handled; or controlled
吾問焉，曰：『操舟可學邪？』
"I asked, saying, 'Can handling of boats be learnt?'
曰：『可。善游者數能。若乃夫沒人，則未嘗見舟而便操之也。』
"And they said, 'It can. Good swimmers are able to upon repetition. If it be a diver, then having not yet seen boats may he immediately handle them.'
  • 數： upon repetition
    數，入聲， Cantonese: shok8, Mandarin: shuò
  • 沒人：沒，沉也； diver; literally submerger
  • 便：即也，輒也； immediately; or soon
吾問焉而不吾告，敢問何謂也？」
"That which I asked, they told me not; I dare ask: what were they saying?"
  • 何謂也： what were they saying; or what did they mean
仲尼曰：「善游者數能，忘水也。
仲尼 (Chung-nei) (Chung-ni) (Zhongni) said, "Good swimmers are able to upon repetition: they forget about the water.
若乃夫沒人之未嘗見舟而便操之也，彼視淵若陵，視舟之覆猶其車卻也。
"Of having not yet seen boats and immediately handling them, if it be a diver: he vieweth the deep waters as an hill, and vieweth the tipping of a boat like his chariot receding.
  • 覆： tipping; lit. overturning
    Probably not capsizing, for that ought to be compared with a chariot rolling over, rather than merely receding.
  • 卻： receding
    Rolling backwards down the slope of said hill.
覆卻萬方陳乎前而不得入其舍，惡往而不暇！
"Tipping and receding have, in a myriad instances, been shown before him and not entered his mind; why goeth he and be he not carefree?
  • 方： instances
    This is following the annotation of 宣穎 (Sün Wing) (Hsüan Ying) (Xuan Ying), which is 方，猶端 (although he may have meant "upright" by that?).
  • 舍： mind; lit. residence; or abode
  • 惡：何也； why
    惡，平聲， Cantonese: woo1, Mandarin: wū
  • 暇： carefree
以瓦注者巧，以鉤注者憚，以黃金注者殙。
"He who wagereth by archery for earthenware is skillful; he who wagereth by archery for a buckle, fearful; and he who wagereth by archery for gold, dazed.
  • 注： wagereth by archery
    Opinion is divided on whether 注 refers to archery. Among the annotations I could be bothered digging up, these say strike:
    • 《南華真經・郭象註》 i.e. 《南華真經》 with annotations by 郭象 (Kwok Tsœng) (Kuo Hsiang) (Guo Xiang)
      注，之樹反，李云擊也。
      注 (chü) (chu) (zhu), hath 反切 (faan ts'eet) (fan ch'ieh) (fan qie) pronunciation 之樹 (chee shü) (chih shu) (zhi shu), and 李 (Lei) (Li) (Li) saith it meaneth strike.
      • 之樹反： hath 反切 (faan ts'eet) (fan ch'ieh) (fan qie) pronunciation 之樹 (chee shü) (chih shu) (zhi shu)
        Demonstrating this in Cantonese:
        • 之 is chee1 (陰平), i.e. initial ch, final ee, tone 陰平
        • 樹 is shü6 (陽去), i.e. initial sh, final ü, tone 陽去
        Therefore their 反切 (faan ts'eet) (fan ch'ieh) (fan qie) has initial ch, final ü, tone 陰去, i.e. chü3.
      • 擊： strike; or hit
    • 《康熙字典》 i.e. 康熙 (Hong-hei) (K'ang-hsi) (Kangxi) Dictionary
      又擊也。
      Also strike.
      Notably, the dictionary uses the line 以黃金注者㱪 from our passage as the example for this meaning.

    These say shoot:

    • 《南華真經註疏》 i.e. 《南華真經》 with annotations and further commentary
      注，射也。
      注, meaneth shoot.
    • 《莊子注》, by 郭象 (Kwok Tsœng) (Kuo Hsiang) (Guo Xiang)
      注，射也，射而賭物曰注。
      注, meaneth shoot; shooting for a wager of objects is called 注.
      射而賭物 is the meaning taken by James Legge (see footnote 1 of Page 16).

    Does 擊, meaning strike or hit, refer to striking or hitting with an arrow? Perhaps.

    In any case though, 注 certainly has the primary meaning of "wager", so I have decided on rendering 注 as "wagereth by archery".

    Here I will point out that the conciseness of Literary Chinese comes with a great cost: no one can really be sure of the author's intended meaning. (Such is also the case for religious texts). Many scholars have written 註, annotations, and 疏, further commentary, or annotations of the annotations. And now I am writing annotations of annotations of annotations...

    • 疏，去聲， Cantonese: shor3, Mandarin: shù, Government-regulated Mandarin (統讀): shū
  • 鉤：帶鉤； buckle; or belt buckle
  • 憚：畏也，忌難也； fearful; or afraid of the difficulty
    憚，音但， Cantonese: taan6, Mandarin: dàn
  • 黃金： gold; lit. yellow gold
  • 殙： dazed; or confused; lit. unclear in vision
    殙，呼昆切， Cantonese: fan1, Mandarin: hūn
    In some versions I have seen this written 㱪 (U+3C6A) (i.e. 歹 next to 昬, which won't show if your browser font doesn't have it in its character set). Again people don't agree on exactly what this means, but I concur with those who say it is a variant of 惛.
其巧一也，而有所矜，則重外也。凡外重者內拙。」
"Their skill is the same, but that there be that which they are concerned about, is attaching importance to the external. All those who attach importance to the external, are internally stupid."
  • 矜： concerned about
    This is very similar to 重 which follows.
  • 重： attaching importance to; lit. treating as heavy
    重，去聲， Cantonese: chung6, Mandarin: zhòng
    The opposite of the verb 重 is the verb 輕, which is to "make light of", or "treat as light".
  • 拙： stupid
    The opposite of 巧, which is "clever" or "skillful".

Comparison

[▲ Translation]

I thought it might be informative to put the three English versions of the passage side by side: mine, James Legge's and Thomas Merton's. Here I remind the reader that Thomas Merton's version is not a translation, but a "free interpretative reading" based on the translations of James Legge and others.

Conway James Legge Thomas Merton
He who wagereth by archery
for earthenware is skillful; 		He who is contending for a
piece of earthenware
puts forth all his skill. 		When an archer is shooting for nothing
He has all his skill.
He who wagereth by archery
for a buckle, fearful; 		If the prize be a buckle of brass,
he shoots timorously; 		If he shoots for a brass buckle
He is already nervous.
And he who wagereth by archery
for gold, dazed. 		If it be for an article of gold,
he shoots as if he were blind. 		If he shoots for a prize of gold
He goes blind
Or sees two targets—
He is out of his mind!
Their skill is the same,
but that there be that
which they are concerned about, 		The skill of the archer is
the same in all the cases;
but (in the two latter cases)
he is under the influence of solicitude, 		His skill has not changed. But the prize
Divides him. He cares.
Is attaching importance
to the external.
All those who attach importance
to the external,
are internally stupid. 		And looks on the external prize
as most important.
All who attach importance
to what is external show
stupidity in themselves. 		He thinks more of winning
Than of shooting—
And the need to win
Drains him of power.
