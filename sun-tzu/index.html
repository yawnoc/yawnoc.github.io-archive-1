Introduction

《孫子算經》, or "Sun Tzŭ's Computational Classic", like the Sea Island Computational Classic, is one of the ten canon classics of Chinese mathematics. Probably written in the 3rd to 5th centuries AD, the author Sun Tzŭ (孫子) is not the same Sun Tzŭ who wrote The Art of War.

In late April 2019, I set myself the rather ambitious task of translating the entire 《孫子算經》 into English. Apparently this has been done before, but I shall not read that translation (which is behind a paywall anyway) until I have completely finished mine.

As of 23 November 2019, I have finished the manuscript for the entire text, but it will probably take until the end of 2020 to type up all of this in web (HTML) format.

Versions

There are four versions which I refer to, with slight differences in wording between them. Unless otherwise noted I will be following the text from Version D. The punctuation is based on that in Version C (ctext.org), but with changes to my liking. I have grouped the paragraphs mostly following the line breaks of Versions A, B and D, with groupings which in my opinion are more logical than those in Version C.

A quick system for referring to specific pages is as follows:

  • A: 02094034.cn at archive.org
    Ap shall mean https://archive.org/details/02094034.cn/page/np
  • B: 《文淵閣四庫全書》第0797冊 at Wikimedia Commons
    Bp(q) shall mean https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?title=File%3A文淵閣四庫全書_0797冊.djvu&page=p
    corresponding to the printed page number q.
    Note that {\colg p} - {\colg q} = 5.
  • C: ctext.org database page
    Cp(v.q) shall mean https://ctext.org/sunzi-suan-jing#np
    corresponding to Volume v, Line q according to ctext.org's numbering.
  • D: 《知不足齋叢書》本 at ctext.org library (This shall be the base reference version.)
    Dp shall mean https://ctext.org/library.pl?if=en&file=86926&page=p

A note on mass and weight

Classical Chinese does not distinguish between mass and weight; nor is this a physics text in which the distinction is important. Therefore I will be using the two terms interchangeably.

A note on thousands and myriads

In Chinese, numbers are grouped in 萬, myriads (10000s), rather than thousands (1000s). For example, 八萬五千 (85000) is literally "eight myriad five thousand" rather than "eighty-five thousand". However, I shall default to rendering numbers in the English using the thousands grouping, only using the myriads grouping for passages where the thousands grouping is awkward.
