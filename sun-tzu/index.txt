<@2> Introduction | introduction </@2>


----
  《孫子算經》, or
  <@>
    "[sun-tzu-classic]"
    | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunzi_Suanjing
    | Wikipedia: Sunzi Suanjing
  </@>,
  like the
  <@>
    Sea Island Computational Classic
    | /lit/sea-island
    | "The Sea Island Computational Classic"
  </@>, is one of the
  <@>
    ten canon classics of Chinese mathematics
    | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ten_Computational_Canons
    | Wikipedia: Ten Computational Canons
  </@>.
  Probably written in the 3rd to 5th centuries AD,
  the author [sun-tzu] (孫子)
  is not the same
  <@>
    [sun-tzu]
    | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Tzu
    | Wikipedia: Sun Tzu
  </@>
  who wrote
  <@>
    {The Art of War}
    | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War
    | Wikipedia: The Art of War
  </@>.
----

----
  In late April~2019, I set myself the rather ambitious task of translating
  the entire 《孫子算經》 into English.
  Apparently
  <@>
    this has been done before
    | https://books.google.com.au/books/about/Fleeting_Footsteps.html\
        ?id=fGYmpWE5UZgC
    | Fleeting Footsteps: \
        Tracing the Conception of Arithmetic and Algebra in Ancient China
  </@>,
  but I shall <strong>not</strong> read that translation
  (which is behind a paywall anyway) until I have completely finished mine.
----

----
  As of 23 November 2019, I have finished the
  <@> manuscript | #manuscript | Manuscript </@> for the entire text,
  but it will probably take until the end of 2020
  to type up all of this in web (HTML) format.
----

<@3> Versions | versions </@3>

----
  There are four versions which I refer to,
  with slight differences in wording between them.
  <strong>Unless otherwise noted I will be following the text from
  <@>
    Version~D
    | https://ctext.org/library.pl?if=en&res=80226
    | 孫子算經+五曹算經（知不足齋本）
  </@>.</strong>
  The punctuation is based on that in
  <@>
    Version~C (ctext.org)
    | https://ctext.org/sunzi-suan-jing
    | 《孫子算經》
  </@>,
  but with changes to my liking.
  I have grouped the paragraphs mostly following the line breaks
  of Versions~A, B and~D, with groupings which
  in my opinion are more logical than those in Version~C.
----

<@3> A note on mass and weight | weight </@3>

----
  Classical Chinese does not distinguish between mass and weight;
  nor is this a physics text in which the distinction is important.
  Therefore I will be using the two terms interchangeably.
----

<@3> A note on thousands and myriads | myriads </@3>

----
  In Chinese, numbers are grouped in 萬, myriads,
  rather than thousands.
  For example, 八萬五千 is literally
  "eight myriad five thousand" rather than "eighty-five thousand".
  However, I shall default to rendering numbers in the English using the
  thousands grouping, only using the myriads grouping
  for passages where the thousands grouping is awkward.
----
